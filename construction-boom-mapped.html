D.C.'s Construction Boom: Mapped

Kate Rabinowitz // January XX, 2016

D.C. is undergoing a construction boom this decade which is dramatically altering the city landscape. To keep up with a growing population and increasing demand for urban living, vacant lots are being built up and apartment buildings are rising up to the height restriction around the city.

More than 1,500 residential building permits issues since 2012

Residential building permits issued, January 2012-January 2016

Since 2012 the D.C. residential construction boom has blanketed much of the city with the exception of historically preserved neighborhoods including Capitol Hill, Dupont Circle and Georgetown. Clusters of new residential buildings appear in the Northwest and Southeast corners of the District, along with Brookland, Edgewood and the corridors of Shaw, Logan Circle, and H St NE.

The building boom looks different across the District. Over 1,000 of the new buildings are single family. These are largely concentrated around the perimeter of the city while much of inner city development is multi family. New buildings in Southwest are almost exclusively apartments, helping to put neighborhoods like Navy Yard on track to be the most densely populated in the city. The H St NE corridor and lower Northwest neighborhoods are a mix of duplexes and larger apartment buildings.

New single family buildings along District perimeter, multi-family in District core

Residential building permits issued by building type, January 2012-January 2016

The dates shown here are for permit issue, which precede construction completion by months or even years for larger projects. With over 30 permits for new residential buildings already issued in 2016, the District landscape will continue to change for some time.
